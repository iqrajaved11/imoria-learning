1.1 What is Biology?

Biology is the branch of science that deals with the study of living organisms and their vital processes. The term "Biology" comes from two Greek words: bios (life) and logos (study).

Definition
"Biology is the branch of natural science concerned with the study of life and living organisms, including their structure, function, growth, origin, evolution, and distribution."

Biology covers a vast range of topics — from the molecular mechanisms within a single cell to the global patterns of ecosystems. It is broadly divided into two main branches:

Botany — the study of plants  |  Zoology — the study of animals

📌
Board Exam Tip "Define Biology and name its two main branches" is a 2-mark question that appears almost every year. Write the Greek origin of the word for full marks.

1.2 Biodiversity

Definition
Biodiversity refers to the variety of life on Earth, including the variety of genes, species, and ecosystems. The term was coined by W.G. Rosen in 1985.

Biodiversity is studied at three main levels:

LevelDescriptionExample
Genetic DiversityVariation in genes within a speciesDifferent eye colours in humans
Species DiversityNumber of different species in an area500 bird species in a rainforest
Ecosystem DiversityVariety of habitats and ecological processesForests, wetlands, deserts
Key Fact: Pakistan has significant biodiversity — over 5,000 plant species, 668 bird species, 195 mammal species, and 177 reptile species.
🧠
Memory Trick Remember "GSE" for the three levels: Genetic → Species → Ecosystem. Think: "Good Students Explore biodiversity."

1.3 Classification & Taxonomy

Taxonomy
Taxonomy is the branch of biology that deals with the nomenclature, identification, and classification of organisms. The father of taxonomy is Carolus Linnaeus (1707–1778), a Swedish botanist.

Carolus Linnaeus introduced the binomial nomenclature system — giving every organism a two-part Latin name consisting of the Genus and species.

Binomial Nomenclature Rules:
• The genus name starts with a capital letter
• The species name is in small letters
• The entire name is written in italics (or underlined when handwritten)
• Example: Homo sapiens (humans), Felis catus (cat)

Classification follows a hierarchical system with 7 main taxonomic ranks:

1.4 Five Kingdom Classification

Robert Whittaker (1969) proposed the Five Kingdom Classification system based on cell type, body organisation, nutrition, and reproduction.

KingdomCell TypeNutritionExamples
MoneraProkaryoticAbsorption / PhotosynthesisBacteria, Cyanobacteria
ProtistaEukaryoticAutotrophic / HeterotrophicAmoeba, Euglena, Paramecium
FungiEukaryoticAbsorption (Saprophytic)Mushrooms, Yeast, Moulds
PlantaeEukaryoticPhotosynthesis (Autotrophic)Mosses, Ferns, Flowering plants
AnimaliaEukaryoticIngestion (Heterotrophic)Sponges, Insects, Fish, Humans
Six Kingdom Classification: Carl Woese (1977) later divided Monera into Archaebacteria and Eubacteria, creating a Six Kingdom system. This is also mentioned in the Punjab Board syllabus.

1.5 Viruses — Are They Living?

Virus
Viruses are acellular (non-cellular), ultramicroscopic infectious agents that can only replicate inside a living host cell. They are not placed in any of the five kingdoms.

The term "virus" was coined by Dmitri Ivanowski (1892). W.M. Stanley first crystallised the tobacco mosaic virus (TMV) in 1935, showing it was non-living outside a host.

A virus consists of:

• Nucleic acid core — either DNA or RNA (never both)
• Protein coat (Capsid) — protects the nucleic acid
• Some have an outer lipid envelope (enveloped viruses)

📌
Board Exam Tip "Why are viruses not placed in any kingdom?" — Answer: Because they are acellular, non-metabolising outside a host, and can be crystallised like non-living matter. This is a popular 2-mark question.

2 Mark Past Paper Define biodiversity and name its three levels. ▼

Biodiversity: Biodiversity refers to the variety of life on Earth at all its levels, from genes to ecosystems. The term was coined by W.G. Rosen in 1985.

Three Levels of Biodiversity:

  1. Genetic Diversity: Variation in genes and genotypes within a species (e.g., different blood groups in humans).
  2. Species Diversity: The variety of different species in a given area or habitat.
  3. Ecosystem Diversity: The range of different habitats, ecological communities, and ecological processes.

2 Mark What is binomial nomenclature? Give two examples. ▼

Binomial Nomenclature: It is the system of giving each organism a scientific name consisting of two parts — the genus (first word, capital letter) and the species (second word, small letter). It was introduced by Carolus Linnaeus.

Rules: The name is written in Latin and printed in italics (underlined in handwriting).

Examples:

2 Mark Past Paper Why are viruses not included in any of the five kingdoms? ▼

Viruses are not included in any kingdom because:

  1. They are acellular (non-cellular) — they have no cell structure.
  2. They are non-metabolising outside a host — they show no life processes independently.
  3. They can be crystallised like non-living substances (as shown by W.M. Stanley in 1935).
  4. They only replicate inside a living host cell — they are obligate intracellular parasites.
Marks: 2
5 Mark Past Paper Describe the Five Kingdom Classification system proposed by Robert Whittaker. ▼

Five Kingdom Classification was proposed by Robert Whittaker in 1969. He classified all living organisms into five kingdoms based on: cell type, body organisation, mode of nutrition, and mode of reproduction.

  1. Kingdom Monera: Prokaryotic, unicellular organisms. Nutrition by absorption or photosynthesis. Examples: Bacteria, Cyanobacteria.
  2. Kingdom Protista: Eukaryotic, mostly unicellular. Nutrition autotrophic or heterotrophic. Examples: Amoeba, Paramecium, Euglena.
  3. Kingdom Fungi: Eukaryotic, mostly multicellular. Saprophytic nutrition by absorption. Examples: Mushrooms, Yeast, Rhizopus.
  4. Kingdom Plantae: Eukaryotic, multicellular. Autotrophic nutrition by photosynthesis. Examples: Mosses, Ferns, Angiosperms.
  5. Kingdom Animalia: Eukaryotic, multicellular. Heterotrophic nutrition by ingestion. Examples: Sponges, Insects, Mammals.
Marks: 5
2 Mark What is taxonomy and who is called the father of taxonomy? ▼

Taxonomy is the branch of biology that deals with the identification, nomenclature, and classification of organisms. The word is derived from Greek: taxis (arrangement) + nomos (law).

Father of Taxonomy: Carolus Linnaeus (1707–1778), a Swedish botanist, is called the Father of Taxonomy. He introduced the binomial nomenclature system and laid the foundation of modern biological classification in his book Systema Naturae (1735).

5 Mark Past Paper Write a comprehensive note on the classification system of Carolus Linnaeus and discuss the significance of binomial nomenclature. ▼

Carolus Linnaeus (1707–1778) was a Swedish botanist who is considered the Father of Taxonomy. He developed a hierarchical system of classification that forms the basis of modern biological classification.

Hierarchical Classification: Linnaeus arranged organisms in a hierarchy from most general to most specific: Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species.

Binomial Nomenclature — introduced by Linnaeus — gives each organism a unique two-part Latin name:

  1. The first part is the Genus name (capital letter).
  2. The second part is the species name (lowercase).

Significance of Binomial Nomenclature:

  1. Provides a universal name understood by scientists worldwide, regardless of language.
  2. Eliminates confusion caused by common names (e.g., "robin" refers to different birds in UK and USA).
  3. Indicates evolutionary relationships between organisms.
  4. The two-part name conveys both the species' identity and its genus, showing relationship to similar organisms.
