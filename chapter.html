Gases & Gas Laws

3.1 — The Kinetic Molecular Theory of Gases

The kinetic molecular theory provides a model to explain the behaviour of ideal gases. It is based on several key postulates that describe gas particles and their motion.

  • Gas consists of a large number of tiny particles (atoms or molecules) that are in constant, rapid, random motion.
  • The volume of gas particles themselves is negligible compared to the total volume of the container.
  • Gas particles exert no attractive or repulsive forces on each other — they interact only during elastic collisions.
  • All collisions between gas particles and the walls of the container are perfectly elastic (no net kinetic energy lost).
  • The average kinetic energy of gas particles is directly proportional to the absolute temperature (in Kelvin).
Key Definition

An ideal gas is a hypothetical gas that perfectly obeys all the postulates of the kinetic molecular theory. Real gases approximate ideal behaviour at high temperatures and low pressures.

3.2 — Gas Laws

Three fundamental laws describe the relationships between pressure, volume, temperature, and amount of gas. Each law isolates two variables while keeping all others constant.

Law Variables Relationship Equation
Boyle's Law P and V (T, n constant) Inversely proportional PV = k (constant)
Charles' Law V and T (P, n constant) Directly proportional V/T = k
Avogadro's Law V and n (P, T constant) Directly proportional V/n = k

3.3 — The Ideal Gas Equation

Combining Boyle's Law, Charles' Law, and Avogadro's Law gives the Ideal Gas Equation, the single most important equation in this chapter and a favourite in board MCQs:

Ideal Gas Equation
PV = nRT
P = pressure (atm) · V = volume (L) · n = moles · R = 0.0821 L·atm/mol·K · T = temperature (K)

The gas constant R = 0.0821 L·atm/mol·K when using atmospheres and litres. If SI units are used (pressure in Pa, volume in m³), then R = 8.314 J/mol·K.

3.4 — Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures

When two or more gases that do not react chemically are mixed in a container, each gas exerts pressure independently as though it occupied the container alone. The total pressure is the sum of the partial pressures of each component.

Dalton's Law
Ptotal = P1 + P2 + P3 + ···
Partial pressure: P₁ = X₁ × Ptotal where X₁ is the mole fraction
Board Exam Tip

Dalton's Law is frequently tested in short questions. Remember: the law applies only to non-reacting gases, and the partial pressure of any component equals its mole fraction multiplied by the total pressure.

3.5 — Graham's Law of Diffusion & Effusion

Graham's Law states that the rate of diffusion or effusion of a gas is inversely proportional to the square root of its molar mass. This means lighter gases diffuse faster than heavier gases at the same temperature and pressure.

3.6 — Real Gases & van der Waals Equation

Real gases deviate from ideal behaviour because: (1) gas particles do have finite volume, and (2) intermolecular attractive forces do exist, especially at high pressures and low temperatures. The van der Waals equation corrects for both these factors.

Short Questions — Chapter 3

Short Q ⭐ Important Past Paper
State Boyle's Law and give its mathematical expression.
▼

Boyle's Law: At constant temperature, the volume of a fixed amount of gas is inversely proportional to its pressure.

Mathematical Expression: P ∝ 1/V, or PV = k (constant) at fixed T and n. For two states of the same gas: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂.

Graphical Representation: A plot of P vs V gives a hyperbola (P × V = constant). A plot of P vs 1/V gives a straight line through the origin.

Board Marks: 2
Short Q ⭐ Important
What is absolute zero? What is its value in Celsius and Kelvin?
▼

Absolute Zero is the lowest theoretically possible temperature — the point at which all molecular motion ceases and a gas would have zero volume (according to Charles' Law).

Value: 0 K = −273.15°C ≈ −273°C. In board exams, use −273°C as the accepted value.

At absolute zero, kinetic energy of gas particles is zero, meaning no molecular motion exists. This is a theoretical limit that cannot be physically reached, only approached.

Board Marks: 2
Short Q Past Paper
Define molar volume of a gas. What is its value at STP?
▼

Molar Volume is the volume occupied by one mole of any ideal gas under specified conditions of temperature and pressure.

At STP (Standard Temperature and Pressure): T = 0°C (273 K), P = 1 atm

Molar volume = 22.4 litres per mole (22.4 L/mol). This value is the same for all ideal gases at STP, regardless of their molecular mass — a consequence of Avogadro's Law.

Board Marks: 2
Short Q
Under what conditions do real gases behave like ideal gases? Give reasons.
▼

Real gases behave most like ideal gases under conditions of high temperature and low pressure.

High Temperature: Molecules move faster with greater kinetic energy, which overcomes intermolecular attractive forces. Attractions become negligible relative to kinetic energy.

Low Pressure: Molecules are far apart, so the volume of molecules themselves is negligible compared to the total volume. The space between molecules approximates an empty container.

Board Marks: 3
Short Q ⭐ Important
State Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures with its equation.
▼

Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures: The total pressure exerted by a mixture of non-reacting gases is equal to the sum of the partial pressures of each individual gas in the mixture.

Equation: P_total = P₁ + P₂ + P₃ + ··· + Pₙ

Where each partial pressure Pᵢ = Xᵢ × P_total, and Xᵢ is the mole fraction of component i. This law holds because each gas behaves independently and occupies the full volume of the container.

Long Questions — Chapter 3

Long Q ⭐ Important Past Paper
State and explain the Kinetic Molecular Theory of gases. How does it explain Boyle's Law and Charles' Law?
▼

Kinetic Molecular Theory (KMT) is a model that describes the behaviour of ideal gases using the following postulates:

1. Gases consist of extremely small particles (molecules or atoms) that are in continuous, rapid, random motion in all directions.
2. The volume of gas particles is negligible compared to the total volume of the container (gas is mostly empty space).
3. Gas particles exert no attractive or repulsive forces on each other except during brief, perfectly elastic collisions.
4. The average kinetic energy of gas particles is directly proportional to the absolute temperature: KE_avg = (3/2)kT.

Explaining Boyle's Law: At constant temperature, kinetic energy (and speed) of molecules remains constant. Decreasing volume means molecules strike the walls more frequently per unit area, increasing pressure. Hence P ∝ 1/V.

Explaining Charles' Law: Increasing temperature increases the average kinetic energy and speed of molecules. They strike the walls harder and more often. To maintain constant pressure, the container must expand, so V ∝ T.

Board Marks: 8
Long Q Past Paper
Derive the Ideal Gas Equation PV = nRT by combining Boyle's Law, Charles' Law, and Avogadro's Law.
▼

Step 1 — Boyle's Law: V ∝ 1/P at constant T and n

Step 2 — Charles' Law: V ∝ T at constant P and n

Step 3 — Avogadro's Law: V ∝ n at constant P and T

Combining: V ∝ nT/P, or V = R × nT/P, where R is the proportionality constant (the universal gas constant).

Rearranging: PV = nRT

Value of R: Using 1 mole of gas at STP: P = 1 atm, V = 22.4 L, n = 1 mol, T = 273 K → R = PV/nT = (1 × 22.4)/(1 × 273) = 0.0821 L·atm/mol·K

Board Marks: 8
Long Q ⭐ Important
Explain how and why real gases deviate from ideal gas behaviour. Discuss the factors that cause deviation and how the van der Waals equation corrects for them.
▼

Ideal Gas Assumptions vs. Reality: The ideal gas model assumes (1) no intermolecular forces and (2) gas particles have no volume. Both assumptions break down for real gases.

Factor 1 — Intermolecular Attractive Forces: Real gas molecules do attract each other. When a molecule approaches the wall, neighbouring molecules pull it back slightly, reducing the pressure it exerts. The measured pressure is less than the ideal pressure. Correction: add a/V² to the pressure term, where 'a' measures the strength of intermolecular attractions.

Factor 2 — Finite Molecular Volume: Gas molecules occupy real space. The free volume available for movement is less than the container volume. Correction: subtract 'nb' from volume, where 'b' is the volume excluded per mole of molecules.

van der Waals Equation: (P + an²/V²)(V − nb) = nRT

This equation gives more accurate results at high pressures and low temperatures, where ideal gas assumptions fail most severely.

Board Marks: 10
