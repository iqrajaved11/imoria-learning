Introduction to Gases

Gases represent one of the three fundamental states of matter. Unlike solids and liquids, gases have no definite shape or volume — they expand to fill any container they occupy. This makes them uniquely predictable through a set of laws that relate pressure, volume, temperature, and amount.

The study of gas behaviour is central to both board exams and MDCAT. This chapter covers the four key gas laws and their mathematical relationships, plus the Kinetic Molecular Theory that explains why gases behave the way they do.

⭐ Important for Board Exam
This chapter carries approximately 10–15% of Chemistry MCQs in board exams. Focus especially on Boyle's Law, Charles' Law, and the Ideal Gas Equation. All four laws appear consistently in past papers.

Boyle's Law

Boyle's Law states that at constant temperature, the pressure of a fixed amount of gas is inversely proportional to its volume.

Discovered by Robert Boyle in 1662, this is the first of the three classical gas laws. It describes how a gas compresses when pressure is applied — like squeezing a balloon.

📐 Mathematical Statement
P ∝ 1/V (at constant T and n)
∴ PV = constant
∴ P₁V₁ = P₂V₂

Key Points — Boyle's Law

  • Applies only at constant temperature (isothermal process)
  • When volume decreases, pressure increases proportionally
  • Graph of P vs V gives a hyperbola (rectangular hyperbola)
  • Graph of P vs 1/V gives a straight line through origin
  • Holds true for ideal gases; real gases deviate at high pressure
💡 Board Tip
In numerical questions, always convert pressure units to the same unit before applying P₁V₁ = P₂V₂. Common units: atm, mmHg (torr), Pa, kPa. Standard pressure = 1 atm = 760 mmHg = 101.325 kPa.

Charles' Law

Charles' Law states that at constant pressure, the volume of a fixed amount of gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature (in Kelvin).

Proposed by Jacques Charles in 1787, this law explains why a balloon expands when heated and contracts when cooled.

📐 Mathematical Statement
V ∝ T (at constant P and n)
∴ V/T = constant
∴ V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂
⚠️ Common Mistake
Always convert °C to Kelvin before using Charles' Law!
T(K) = T(°C) + 273.15
Using °C directly is a very common error in board exams.

Absolute Zero

Charles' Law predicts that gas volume becomes zero at −273.15°C, which corresponds to 0 Kelvin — called absolute zero. In reality, no gas behaves ideally at such low temperatures, but this concept defines the absolute temperature scale.

Gay-Lussac's Law

Gay-Lussac's Law states that at constant volume, the pressure of a fixed amount of gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature.

📐 Mathematical Statement
P ∝ T (at constant V and n)
∴ P/T = constant
∴ P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂

Real-World Application

  • A sealed pressure cooker: as temperature rises, pressure inside increases
  • Car tyres in summer: higher temperature → higher tyre pressure
  • Aerosol cans: never expose to heat — Gay-Lussac's Law explains why

Combined Gas Law

The three laws above can be combined into a single equation when all three variables (P, V, T) change simultaneously, while the amount of gas remains fixed:

📐 Combined Gas Law
PV/T = constant
∴ P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂

Note that each individual gas law is a special case of the Combined Gas Law:

  • Boyle's Law: T is constant → cancels out on both sides
  • Charles' Law: P is constant → cancels out on both sides
  • Gay-Lussac's Law: V is constant → cancels out on both sides

Ideal Gas Equation

The Ideal Gas Law combines all four variables — pressure, volume, temperature, and amount (in moles) — into one unified equation. This is the most important equation in this chapter.

📐 Ideal Gas Equation
PV = nRT

P = pressure (in atm or Pa)
V = volume (in L or m³)
n = moles of gas
R = universal gas constant
T = absolute temperature (Kelvin)

Value of R (Universal Gas Constant)

Unit System Value of R Use When
SI (standard) 8.314 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹ P in Pa, V in m³
Practical 0.0821 L·atm mol⁻¹ K⁻¹ P in atm, V in litres (most common)
Calorie 1.987 cal mol⁻¹ K⁻¹ Thermochemistry calculations
⭐ MDCAT Focus
PV = nRT is the single most tested equation in MDCAT Chemistry. You must be able to rearrange it to find any one variable: n = PV/RT, T = PV/nR, V = nRT/P, P = nRT/V. Practice all four forms.

Kinetic Molecular Theory

The Kinetic Molecular Theory (KMT) provides a microscopic model to explain why gases follow the laws described above. It is based on five fundamental postulates:

  1. Gases consist of tiny particles (atoms or molecules) that are in constant, random motion.
  2. Gas particles have negligible volume compared to the total volume of the gas. The gas is mostly empty space.
  3. No intermolecular forces exist between gas particles — they neither attract nor repel each other.
  4. Collisions are perfectly elastic — no net kinetic energy is lost during particle collisions.
  5. Temperature is proportional to average kinetic energy — higher temperature means faster-moving particles.
📐 Average Kinetic Energy
KE_avg = (3/2)kT
k = Boltzmann constant = 1.38 × 10⁻²³ J K⁻¹
T = absolute temperature (Kelvin)

KMT — How It Explains Gas Laws

Boyle's Law:
Decreasing volume → more frequent collisions with walls → higher pressure.
Charles' Law:
Higher temperature → faster particles → more forceful collisions → larger volume needed to maintain constant pressure.
Gay-Lussac's:
Higher temperature → faster particles → more frequent collisions with fixed walls → higher pressure.

Deviations from Ideal Behaviour

Real gases deviate from ideal gas behaviour under conditions of:

  • High pressure — gas particles are forced close together; volume of particles can no longer be neglected
  • Low temperature — particles slow down; intermolecular forces become significant
💡 Memory Trick
Ideal gas behaviour is closest at low pressure and high temperature — conditions where gas particles are far apart and moving fast enough to overcome any intermolecular attractions.

van der Waals Equation (for Real Gases)

The van der Waals equation corrects the ideal gas equation for real gas behaviour by adding two correction factors:

📐 van der Waals Equation
(P + an²/V²)(V − nb) = nRT

a = intermolecular force correction
b = volume correction (excluded volume per mole)
